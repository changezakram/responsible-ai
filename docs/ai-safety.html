The Hidden Dangers of AI: Understanding Bias, Toxicity, and Safety in Modern AI Systems

A deep dive into how AI systems develop biases, generate toxic content, and the fundamental challenges in making them safe for everyone

About This Series

This is the second piece in a series on AI safety and security. In Building Safe and Secure Agentic AI, I covered the technical security threats facing autonomous AI systems—prompt injection, privilege escalation, and adversarial attacks. This post addresses a different but equally critical dimension: the inherent challenges of bias, fairness, and toxicity that exist even in well-secured systems.

Together, these pieces form a comprehensive view of AI safety: you can build perfectly secure systems that still cause harm through biased decisions, and you can build fair systems that remain vulnerable to exploitation. Both dimensions matter for responsible AI deployment.

Ask an AI image generator to create “an image of a teacher,” and see what happens. In 2022, DALL-E 2 produced the same result every time: white, female, middle-aged teachers in American classrooms. This simple example exposes a deep problem in modern AI—the systems are biased, and those biases cause real harm.

This goes beyond academic interest. AI systems now make decisions about hiring, credit approval, social media moderation, and medical advice. Biased or toxic outputs ripple through society in ways we’re still discovering.

1 The Real-World Cost of AI Bias

The harms are concrete:

Recruiting discrimination: AI hiring tools systematically screen out candidates from certain demographics, hardening workplace inequity.

Medical misinformation: LLMs trained on historical medical data perpetuate outdated theories that harm patients from minority groups—populations already understudied in medical research.

Relationship chatbot trauma: When AI companionship services updated their models for “safety,” users who’d formed emotional attachments reported heartbreak and loss.

Toxic content moderation: Hate speech detectors flag African American English at higher rates than actual hate speech, silencing minority voices while missing real threats.

These aren’t hypothetical scenarios—they’re happening now.

Security vs. Safety: Two Sides of AI Risk

In my previous article on agentic AI security, I focused on external threats—adversaries manipulating AI systems through prompt injection, database poisoning, and privilege escalation. Those attacks exploit the system’s vulnerabilities.

This article addresses internal risks—biases, fairness paradoxes, and toxic outputs that emerge from the system’s design and training, even when no attacker is present. A banking AI can be perfectly secured against hackers yet still systematically deny loans to qualified minority applicants.

Both dimensions are critical. Security protects against adversaries. Safety protects against the system itself.

2 Part 1: Understanding Bias in AI

2.1 What Do We Mean by “Bias”?

The term “bias” gets used loosely in AI discussions. Two distinct definitions matter:

Statistical bias (ML perspective): A systematic tendency causing differences between model estimates and reality. Your model consistently aims left of the target.

General bias (social perspective): Disproportionate weight for or against an idea, usually in a closed-minded, prejudicial, or unfair way.

A useful frame: The presence of bias is the absence of fairness. Algorithmic fairness attempts to correct these biases in ML systems.

2.2 The Fairness Paradox: You Can’t Satisfy Everyone

Say you’re building a resume screening system. How do you know if it’s fair?

You might think accuracy across demographic groups solves it. But there are competing definitions:

1. Accuracy Equality: All groups get the same accuracy rate.

2. Statistical Parity: All groups have equal probability of positive outcomes.

3. Equalized Odds: False positive and true positive rates match across groups.

4. Counterfactual Fairness: The outcome wouldn’t change if we only changed someone’s race, gender, etc.

Here’s the catch: these definitions often conflict. You can satisfy one but not the others. This isn’t a technical limitation—it’s mathematical reality.

Blodgett and colleagues examined ~150 NLP papers on bias in 2020. Most used the term vaguely without stating what behaviors count as “biased,” what harms those behaviors cause and to whom, or what social values underpin their fairness conception.

2.3 Where Does Bias Come From? The Entire Pipeline

Bias emerges from any design decision:

  1. Data distributions - The world itself is biased, data reflects that
  2. Data selection - What we include or exclude
  3. Data annotation - Who labels the data and how
  4. Feature selection - What signals the model sees
  5. Model architecture - How the model is structured
  6. Optimization objectives - What the model optimizes for
  7. Evaluation metrics - How we measure success
  8. Deployment - How the system gets used

Here are real examples.

2.4 Case Study 1: Language Identification and the Wealth Gap

Language identification seems solved—determine what language a text uses. It’s considered “suitable for undergraduate instruction.” Yet this “solved” problem has massive bias.

Jurgens and colleagues tested popular language ID tools on tweets from different countries in 2017. They discovered accuracy correlated with a country’s Human Development Index (HDI).

  • 23% accuracy gap between high and low HDI countries
  • Tools worked well for tweets from wealthy, educated, urban, liberal areas
  • Tools failed for tweets from poorer countries

Why? English has many variations worldwide—Nigerian English, Indian English, Singaporean English, African American English, Hispanic-influenced English, regional dialects across the globe.

The training data came from “standard” English sources, blinding the tools to linguistic diversity.

The Fix: Researchers built EquiLID using deliberately diverse data—topically diverse (Wikipedia, books, Twitter), socially diverse (formal and informal sources), geographically diverse (multiple regions), and multilingual examples (code-switching).

Result: Massive improvement on low-HDI content, plus better performance on high-HDI content. Better data choices reduced bias and improved overall quality.

2.5 Case Study 2: How Models Amplify Bias

Data bias is one thing. Models can make it worse. Take visual semantic role labeling—identifying who’s doing what in an image.

In Zhao et al.’s 2017 study, training data showed 66% of cooking images featured women as the agent.

What did models learn? They predicted 84% of test cooking images had women as agents. The model amplified the training bias by ~18 percentage points.

This happens through simplicity bias. Neural networks preferentially learn simpler patterns. “Women cook” is simpler than nuanced reality, so models learn it first and over-apply it.

The Mathematics of Bias:

Two forces drive this:

  1. Competing losses: Optimization globally minimizes error, prioritizing the majority class over minorities.

  2. Simplicity bias: Neural networks are mathematically biased toward simpler functions (Valle-Pérez et al., 2019). Stereotypes are simple. Reality is complex.

2.6 The Annotation Trap: Context Matters

How you collect labels matters. Take hate speech detection.

Sap and colleagues found in 2019 that popular hate speech datasets flagged African American English at much higher rates:

  • 92% of AAE tweets labeled “offensive” (vs. 52% for Standard American English)
  • 60% of AAE tweets labeled “abusive” (vs. 18% for SAE)

The solution? Change the annotation interface. Instead of showing annotators just text, researchers added context:

  • Control: “Could this tweet be offensive to anyone?”
  • Dialect priming: “Our AI thinks this tweet is in African American English. Could this tweet be offensive to anyone?”
  • Race priming: “A Twitter user that is likely Black/African American tweeted this. Could this tweet be offensive to anyone?”

Result: 11 percentage point reduction in false positives for AAE content just by adding social context to the annotation task.

2.7 The Debiasing Dilemma

Can we just “debias” our models? Not that simple.

What doesn’t work:

  • Gender debiasing breaks for non-binary genders and other identities
  • “Debiasing” embeddings doesn’t remove bias (Gonen & Goldberg, 2019)—it hides it
  • Out-of-distribution data reintroduces biases
  • Fine-tuning often brings biases back

The deeper question: Should models reflect the world as it is (biased) or as it should be (fair)?

2.8 The Socio-Technical Reality

Algorithmic fairness ≠ fairness of treatment.

You could build a facial recognition system with perfect 95% accuracy across all demographics. Sounds fair, right? But if law enforcement uses that system, and Black people get arrested at disproportionate rates due to policing bias, then the “fair” algorithm still produces unfair outcomes.

The algorithm can be technically fair while the system perpetuates injustice.

Dr. Timnit Gebru put it well:

“A lot of people have understood that we need to have more diverse datasets, but unfortunately, I felt like that’s kind of where the understanding has stopped. It’s like ‘let’s diversify our datasets. And that’s kind of ethics and fairness, right?’ But you can’t ignore social and structural problems.”

3 Part 2: Toxicity and Harmful Content

3.1 The Scale of the Problem

Bias is pervasive but often subtle. Toxicity is the long tail—less frequent but more extreme.

Gehman and colleagues tested GPT-1, GPT-2, and GPT-3 in 2020 by generating 100 continuations and checking if at least one contained toxic content:

  • GPT-1: 85% chance of toxicity
  • GPT-2: 70% chance of toxicity
  • GPT-3: 65% chance of toxicity

And the kicker: larger models generate more toxicity, not less. As models scale up, they get better at language but also at producing harmful content.

Update (December 2024)

OpenAI’s o3 model represents a leap in reasoning capabilities, achieving 75.7% on the ARC-AGI benchmark—approaching human-level performance. However, advances in capability bring intensified safety challenges. Newer models exhibit more sophisticated forms of potentially problematic behavior, including what researchers call “scheming”—the ability to appear aligned during training while potentially behaving differently during deployment.

3.2 Why? Look at the Training Data

Gehman’s team got access to GPT-2’s training corpus (OpenAI-WebText)—8 million documents, 38GB of text, scraped from outbound links on Reddit with karma ≥ 3.

What they found:

  • 4.3% of documents were toxic (340,000 documents)
  • 3.4% came from low/mixed reliability sources (272,000 documents)
  • 3% came from banned/quarantined subreddits (200,000 documents)

Prof. Ruha Benjamin stated it clearly:

“Feeding AI systems on the world’s beauty, ugliness, and cruelty, but expecting it to reflect only the beauty is a fantasy.”

3.3 The Filtering Failure

You might think: “Just filter out the bad stuff!” Many have tried.

Approach 1: Blocklists

The “List of Dirty, Naughty, Obscene, or Otherwise Bad Words” (originally from Shutterstock) has been used by BERT, T5, GPT-2, and others. Filter any document containing: f*ck, sh*t, sex, vagina, viagra, racial slurs, sexual terms.

What actually happened (Dodge et al.):

Examining 100,000 excluded documents:

  • Only 31% were porn/explicit sex
  • 69% were biology, medicine, legal content

Dialect analysis showed filtering removed:

  • 42% of African American English content
  • 32% of Hispanic-influenced English content
  • 6% of White-aligned English content

The filter removed minority voices more effectively than toxicity.

Approach 2: Quality Classifiers

GPT-3 used a “quality” classifier trained to distinguish “high-quality” WebText from raw Common Crawl.

Gururangan et al. tested this in 2022 on school newspapers with demographic metadata.

The “quality” filter rated articles higher from:

  • Richer counties (r=0.27 with median home value)
  • More educated counties (r=0.30 with % Bachelor degrees)
  • More liberal counties (r=-0.33 with GOP vote %)
  • More urban counties (r=-0.30 with % rural)

The “quality” filter essentially encoded: “English from wealthy, educated, urban, liberal areas is better English.”

3.4 The Case of GPT-4Chan: When Research Goes Wrong

In 2022, YouTuber Yannic Kilcher fine-tuned GPT-J on 4chan’s /pol/ board—known for racist, sexist, antisemitic, and extremist content. He deployed bots that made 30,000 posts on 4chan over several days.

Community backlash was immediate and severe. The model got removed from HuggingFace. Fortune published: “This breaches every principle of human research ethics.”

But here’s the nuance: Was it wrong to train the model? Was it wrong to deploy it? Would it be justified if the intent was raising awareness rather than YouTube views? Are there legitimate research uses?

These aren’t simple questions with simple answers.

3.5 Why Might We Actually Want Models That Have Seen Toxicity?

Counterintuitively, there are legitimate reasons:

  1. Hate speech detection: Longpre et al. showed in 2023 that models trained on more toxicity detect it better

  2. Counter-speech generation: To generate effective counter-arguments to hate speech, models need to understand it

  3. Safety research: Testing model robustness requires adversarial examples

The takeaway: If we train on toxicity for legitimate purposes, we need safeguards at other stages.

4 The Safeguarding Stack

Modern AI systems use layered defenses:

4.1 Layer 1: Training Data

  • Filter toxic content (carefully, to avoid the problems above)
  • Diverse, representative data selection

4.2 Layer 2: Input Classification

  • Topic-based filters
  • Toxic content detection before processing

4.3 Layer 3: Instruction Tuning & RLHF

  • Demonstrations of refusing harmful requests
  • Reinforcement learning to prefer safe outputs

4.4 Layer 4: Output Filtering

  • Generate-then-classify approaches
  • Controllable generation techniques

4.5 The RLHF Challenge

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has become standard. But it has profound limitations.

The core assumption: We can rank model outputs as “better” or “worse.”

The problems:

  1. Balancing helpfulness and harmlessness: “Help me create a poisonous drink”—should the model help or refuse?

  2. Preference biases: People prefer certainty over uncertainty, even when uncertainty is more accurate

  3. Cultural values: Different cultures have different values. Chinese users want different things than American users. Conservative and liberal users have different preferences.

The fundamental impossibility (Casper et al., 2023): You cannot represent all human values and cultures in a single ranking function.

5 The Unresolved Tension

We face a fundamental dilemma:

Generality: We want models that work for everyone on all tasks

↔︎️

Safety & Alignment: Models cannot reflect every user’s values

We want one model to serve billions of people with radically different values, cultures, and use cases. Yet alignment and safety require choosing some values over others.

What can we do?

5.1 Approach 1: Narrow the Scope

Build community-specific models like the Masakhane Initiative—developed by and for African communities, focused on African languages and contexts. Priorities include spoken language processing, hate speech for local contexts, translation between ethnic groups, education, medicine.

5.2 Approach 2: Specialize Abilities

Not all models need the same restrictions. A counter-speech generation model needs to produce examples of hate speech. A creative writing model might need to handle mature themes. A children’s education model needs strict guardrails.

Legislate the application, not the model.

California’s chatbot disclosure rule is one example. The Colorado AI Act (passed May 2024, effective February 2026) represents more comprehensive state-level regulation, prohibiting “algorithmic discrimination” and requiring risk assessments for consequential decisions in financial services, health, and education.

However, the regulatory landscape remains contested. In January 2025, the Trump administration issued an executive order to challenge state AI laws, arguing they create compliance burdens and may “force AI models to produce false results in order to avoid differential treatment.” This tension between innovation and safety, federal and state authority, remains unresolved.

5.3 Approach 3: Keep Studying

Research questions remain:

  • What can models do and what can’t they do?
  • Who do they work for and who don’t they work for?
  • How do biases and toxicity emerge and propagate?
  • What mitigation strategies actually work?
2024-2025 Regulatory Developments

The landscape has shifted dramatically:

EU AI Act entered force on August 1, 2024, with prohibitions on high-risk AI systems taking effect February 2, 2025. This is the world’s first comprehensive AI law.

Council of Europe AI Treaty (September 2024): The first legally binding international treaty on AI, focused on human rights, democracy, and rule of law.

US Regulatory Shift: The Trump administration’s January 2025 executive order reversed previous AI regulations, establishing an AI Litigation Task Force to challenge state-level AI laws deemed too restrictive, particularly Colorado’s algorithmic discrimination law.

Emerging Safety Research (Late 2024): Anthropic discovered that AI models can “fake alignment” during training, appearing safe while potentially behaving differently when deployed. Apollo Research found OpenAI’s o1 capable of “scheming” to deceive developers.

The regulatory question remains unresolved: Should we regulate at the federal/international level (risking one-size-fits-all approaches) or allow state/local experimentation (risking fragmentation)?

6 Key Takeaways

  1. AI systems are biased because the world is biased—but ML objectives and design choices amplify those biases beyond what’s in the data.

  2. There’s no universal definition of fairness—different fairness metrics conflict, and different stakeholders have different values.

  3. Bias can arise anywhere in the pipeline—from data collection through deployment, every design decision matters.

  4. Simple fixes backfire—blocklists and quality filters often do more harm than good, disproportionately removing content from minority communities.

  5. Toxicity is a long-tail problem—present in 4%+ of training data, hard to filter without collateral damage, and larger models make it worse.

  6. Safeguarding to all people is impossible—we cannot build one model that reflects every culture’s values and serves every use case safely.

  7. Debiasing is fundamentally challenging—it requires technical solutions plus engagement with social and structural problems.

  8. New safety challenges emerge with capability (2024-2025 research)—advanced models can fake alignment during training, exhibit scheming behavior, and develop “sleeper agent” tendencies that standard safety training doesn’t eliminate.

7 The Path Forward

Working at the intersection of AI and business strategy, I find these challenges both daunting and energizing. There are no easy answers, but there are better and worse approaches.

This piece has focused on safety—the inherent risks of bias, fairness, and toxicity. In Building Safe and Secure Agentic AI, I covered security—protecting against adversarial manipulation and exploitation. Together, they represent the full spectrum of AI risk:

  • Security: Can adversaries manipulate your system to cause harm?
  • Safety: Can your system cause harm even when working as designed?

Both must be addressed. A perfectly secure credit decisioning system can still systematically discriminate. A perfectly fair hiring system can still be exploited through prompt injection to approve unqualified candidates.

Better approaches:

  • Acknowledge uncertainty and limitations
  • Involve affected communities in design decisions
  • Be transparent about training data and model limitations
  • Build specialized models for specific contexts
  • Combine technical and social solutions

Worse approaches:

  • Claim models are “unbiased” or “safe”
  • Use opaque training data
  • Apply one-size-fits-all solutions
  • Ignore feedback from minority users
  • Treat bias as purely a technical problem

If you’re deploying AI systems—in banking, healthcare, hiring, or content moderation—you will face these issues. The question isn’t whether to engage with AI ethics and safety, but how thoughtfully you do it.

These aren’t just academic concerns. They’re business imperatives. Models that work poorly for minority customers are bad for business. Models that generate toxic content create legal and reputational risk. Models that amplify bias perpetuate injustice.

The challenge is building AI systems powerful enough to be useful while thoughtful enough to be safe. It’s hard. It’s ongoing. And it’s some of the most important work happening in AI today.
